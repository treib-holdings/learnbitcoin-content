Mt. Gox was the first Bitcoin catastrophe big enough to teach the whole market a lesson it keeps forgetting. The site began in 2007 as a trading post for Magic: The Gathering cards - the name is short for "Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange" - and was rebuilt into a Bitcoin exchange in 2010. Mark Karpeles bought it in 2011, and by 2013 it was handling roughly 70 percent of all Bitcoin trades on earth.

On 7 February 2014 it froze withdrawals. On 28 February it filed for bankruptcy in Tokyo. Roughly 850,000 BTC were gone - about 750,000 belonging to customers and 100,000 to the company, close to 7 percent of every bitcoin that existed at the time. There was no single dramatic hack. The coins had been bleeding out for years through poor controls and undetected theft, masked by faked balances. In March 2014 the company turned up 199,999 BTC in an old wallet it had forgotten about, which tells you how loosely the place was run.

Karpeles was convicted in a Japanese court in 2019 - of manipulating data to inflate balances, not of stealing the coins, a charge he was acquitted of. The bankruptcy was later converted into a civil rehabilitation so that creditors would be repaid in bitcoin rather than its 2014 dollar value. Those repayments finally began in July 2024. People who had handed their coins to Mt. Gox waited more than ten years to get a fraction of them back. "Not your keys, not your coins" starts here.
